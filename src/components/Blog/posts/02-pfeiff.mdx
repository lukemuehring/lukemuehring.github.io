The Pfeifferhorn is one of Salt Lake City's most iconic peaks of granite. Located along the Little Cottonwood Canyon ridgeline, the summit rises to 11,326 ft, which is kind of crazy since [the trailhead](https://www.google.com/maps/place/White+Pine+Trailhead/@40.5755164,-111.6836077,1199m/data=!3m2!1e3!4b1!4m6!3m5!1s0x87527a64f4d8b599:0x24210b6d6f17da0c!8m2!3d40.5755164!4d-111.6810328!16s%2Fg%2F11bwh5mcqx?entry=ttu&g_ep=EgoyMDI2MDEyMS4wIKXMDSoASAFQAw%3D%3D) is just a 20 minute drive from my spot in Cottonwood Heights. The easiest way up is hiking the summer route up the canyon and along the eastern ridge, which has 3rd class scrambling just before the summit. Before this trip, I had done that route twice already - first on a mission to stash supplies for the WURL in 2024, and then on the actual WURL itself. 

\
In 2025, I developed some elbow tendonitis, which prevented me from technical rock climbing for several months. It's the longest break I've taken from climbing since I began in 2012. Climbing was always my favorite way to counterbalance the abstract stresses of school and work. It's easy for me to not think about anything else except climbing when I'm on the wall. Without it, I became restless for a new outlet.

\
I got into ridgeline scrambling. It's a pretty funny hybrid between hiking and climbing. You're not exactly climbing, but you're not just walking either. That gray area keeps the movement interesting enough to demand focus, especially when there are consequences. Without the security of a rope, scrambling constantly demands sound judgment on where to draw the line between 4th class and wanton free soloing. 

\
Leaving the rope behind has its benefits. It means moving faster with a smaller pack and less weight, the simplicity of not having to depend on anyone for plans, and the experience of traveling continuously over a giant alpine playground. There's no explicit beta on which holds to use - usually it's on a more macro scale, like which gulley or crack system to follow. Although it's certainly more risky than climbing with a partner, I love how scrambling is a super accesible way of having an adventure.

\
I decided to try the North Ridge in late August, when the snow was completely melted away. Given the 5.6 rating on Mountain Project, I was a bit nervous on what the exposure would be like. But I had done the WURL just a year before. I had also previously been on the South Ridge of Superior, the West Slabs, and more technical scrambles like Geurt's Ridge on Mount Olympus.

### Approach from Maybird Gulch
I started from the White Pine trailhead and eventually took the fork west into Maybird Gulch. I brought camping stuff so I could spend more time out there - I heard that Maybird was more secluded than White Pine. And as far as I could tell, I had the place to myself that Saturday night. I also noticed that one lake still remained this late into the summer.

The next morning, I left camp and followed a trail south until I hit the giant talus field. I took the path of least resistance through it and aimed for the southernmost saddle of the ridgeline.  
Aside from a giant piece teetering unexpectedly, I made it up there without incident. It always excites me when I reach the point where the terrain begins to get more technical than just hiking. It foreshadows the scrambling ahead.
Now that I was on the actual ridge, I could see that no one else was scrambling above, which was a relief. Previous trip reports mentioned that the top had tons of loose rock. I looked down into the moraines below, which appeared as if gigantic monster worms had burrowed into the mountain.

### First Crux
The ridge began with mellow scrambling until the first set of cruxes. [The main photo on SummitPost](https://www.summitpost.org/north-ridge/164300) for this route is a very useful topo. From reading it, I knew there were two tricky spots in the first half of the ridge. First is the rappel. I chose to down climb climber's left. I remember it being exposed but easy 4th class to navigate. 

### Second Crux
Pretty soon after that was the crux of the entire ridge, where I could have made a serious mistake. It involves a traverse around a gendarme (rock tower). I wasn't exactly sure where it was, or which gendarme to look for but soon after the first crux, I found myself blocked by a 15-20 ft. arete. I always prefer sticking true to the ridgeline on top of the knife edge as long as it goes, so I found a stance where I could check out the moves. They looked around v3-v4, and given the exposure, any error falling to the left or right could have resulted in my demise. I decided that there was no way I could responsibly pull the moves, so I bailed off the death spine. It's a decision I'm proud of. Although I'm very stupid for not realizing I was off route in the first place. It's a consequence of doing these ridges without a guide for the first time. Messing up the route finding can put me in serious trouble. This ridge was more serious than the other the ridges I had done in that there were 5th class sections that either had to be solo'd or circumnavigated. 

\
I downclimbed 15 ft. climber's left (east) and found what actually turned out to be the traverse. Woo! Back on track. The old piton was still there, although no fixed line. And still, the traverse was sketchy. There were limited handhold options on the 20 ft. slab, with a lip below that led to an absolute void. On the bright side, it was slabby enough to go slow, and figure it out. It wasn't crazy difficult - I would rate it ~V0-1 slab, but I was smearing with approach shoes, which helps a lot. The most challenging part was that there were no jugs to offer the mental security of 3 solid points on the wall.

\
The rest of the ridge was not as stressful in terms of exposed technical climbing, and was really awesome scrambling in a unique playground of slabs, chimneys, and offwidth to finger cracks. The only other tricky spot for me was after the big platform below the "upper crux". It's a pretty cool spot. The path from there is very choose-your-own-adventure so if things ever get too exposed or hard, there's usually an easier way if you hop around.

\
It's hard to grade scrambles but I've seen the West Slabs and Geurt's go for 5.4 and 5.5, so it makes sense that this is graded as a step up. I wouldn't say there's mandatory 5.6 climbing on this ridge, but you definitely have to be more locked in because of the exposure, route finding, and choss hazard.

\
The rock felt amazing for most of the route, and wasn't looser than what I would expect from a ridge. But as others have noted, the last 100-200 ft on the summit is a minefield of loose rock ranging from watermelon to trash can size scree/talus. I felt that one rock moving would domino effect into the whole mound into shifting. You could very easily cause a rock slide here and I wouldn't want to be below anyone on this section. I'd much rather wait on the big platform before the upper crux.


Next time I got to come back to explore the "Maybird Palisades" and find a way to attack Thunderbolt Ridge in Hogum!
