The Pfeifferhorn is one of Salt Lake City's most iconic peaks of granite, rising 11,326 feet on the Little Cottonwood Canyon ridgeline. The easiest route to the top is the popular summer route along the eastern ridge. It's more of a hike, but it does require some mandatory 3rd and 4th class scrambling just before the summit that [has recently proven to be fatal](https://www.ksl.com/article/51348308/man-dies-in-little-cottonwood-canyon-rockslide). Before this trip, I had summited the Pfeifferhorn twice before, both times via the eastern ridge. The first was on a mission to stash supplies for the WURL in 2024, and th next time was on the actual WURL itself. 

 I was surprised on how easy it was to access [the trailhead](https://www.google.com/maps/place/White+Pine+Trailhead/@40.5755164,-111.6836077,1199m/data=!3m2!1e3!4b1!4m6!3m5!1s0x87527a64f4d8b599:0x24210b6d6f17da0c!8m2!3d40.5755164!4d-111.6810328!16s%2Fg%2F11bwh5mcqx?entry=ttu&g_ep=EgoyMDI2MDEyMS4wIKXMDSoASAFQAw%3D%3D) - it's just a 20 minute drive from my place in Salt Lake City. 

In 2025, I developed some elbow tendonitis, which prevented me from technical rock climbing for several months. It's the longest break I've taken from climbing since I started back in 2012. Besides being a great workout, climbing has been my favorite way to counterbalance the abstract stresses of academics and career. I don't think about anything else when I'm climbing, and that's why I love it. I became restless without it.

As a result, I got into ridgeline scrambling. It's a pretty funny hybrid between hiking and climbing. Without the security of a rope, scrambling constantly demands sound judgment on where to draw the line between class 4 and the wanton free solo. There's no explicit beta on which holds to use or which gulley to follow. But leaving the rope behind has its benefits. It means moving faster with less weight and a smaller pack, the freedom of not having to depend on anyone for plans, and the experience of moving continuously over a giant alpine playground. That's what I love about it - it's one of the most accesible forms of adventure for someone like me. 
\
Anyways, I decided to solo scramble this thing in late August, when the snow was completely melted away. Given the 5.6 rating on Mountain Project, I was a bit nervous on what the exposure would be like. But I had done the WURL just a year before, and worked my way up from the South Ridge of Superior and the West Slabs to more technical scrambles like Geurt's Ridge on Mount Olympus. Combined with over a decade's worth of clmimbing competition experience, I felt adequately ready to navigate both the cruxes and choss.

I hiked in the previous evening from the White Pine trailhead and took the fork to continue west into Maybird Gulch. As far as I could tell, I had the place to myself that Saturday night. I also noticed that one lake still had water, but the others had dried up.

The next morning, I left camp and followed the trail south until I hit the giant talus field. I took the path of least resistance through the rock and aimed for the southernmost saddle of the ridgeline. It always gets my heart racing a bit to realize the terrain is getting more technical. I feel like it foreshadows the moves to come. But aside from a giant piece of talus teetering unexpectedly, I made it up there without incident. Now that I was on the actual ridge, I was relieved that no one else was scrambling above. I could take my time. I looked down into the moraines below, which appeared as if gigantic monster worms had burrowed into the mountain.

The ridge begins with mellow scrambling until the first set of cruxes. [The main photo on SummitPost](https://www.summitpost.org/north-ridge/164300) for this route is a very useful topo. From reading it, I knew there were two tricky spots in the first half of the ridge. First is the rappel. I chose to down climb climber's left. I remember it being exposed but easy 4th class to navigate. Pretty soon after that was the crux of the entire ridge, where I could have made a serious mistake. It involves a traverse around a gendarme (rock tower). I wasn't exactly sure where it was, or which gendarme to look for. When I reached what I later realized was the crux, I found myself blocked by a 15-20 ft. arete. I always prefer sticking true to the ridgeline and staying on top of the knife edge as long as it goes, so I found a stance where I could check out the moves. They looked around v3-v4, and given the exposure, any error falling to the left or right would surely result in my demise. I decided that there was no way I could responsibly pull the moves and that the death spine was on "some Alex Honnold shit", so I bailed. It's a decision I'm proud of. Although I'm very stupid for not realizing I was off route, which also happened to me on the WURL and *also* could have easily been not great for me.

Unlike most of the other ridges I've done, the Pfeifferhorn North Ridge had non-scramblable sections that forced me to either free solo or circumnavigate them. 

This is also a consequence of doing these ridges solo, and unguided for the first time. Messing up the route finding can put me in serious trouble. 

I made an improper decision on the WURL, and I think making the choice to bail really ties together this story of learning where to draw the line.

I downclimbed 15 ft. climber's left (east) and found what actually turned out to be the traverse. Woo! Back on track. The old piton was still there, although no fixed line. And still, the traverse was sketchy. There were limited handhold options with a 20 ft. slab run out below that led to an absolute void. On the bright side, it was slabby enough to go slow, and figure it out. It wasn't crazy difficult - I would give it ~V0-1 slab, but I was smearing with approach shoes, which helps a lot. The most challenging part was that there were no jugs to offer psychological 3-points-on-the-wall security.

The rest of it was not as stressful in terms of exposed technical climbing, and was really awesome scrambling. The only other tricky spot for me was after the big platform below the "upper crux". It's very choose-your-own-adventure so if things ever get too exposed or hard, there's usually an easier way if you keep looking.

It's hard to grade scrambles but I've seen the West Slabs and Geurt's go for 5.4 and 5.5. I also wouldn't say there's mandatory 5.6 climbing on this ridge, but you definitely have to be more locked in because of the exposure, route finding, and choss hazard.





It was an awesome and exposed ridgeline. From the scrambles I've done before (West Slabs, Superior's south ridge, Geurt's Ridge, the WURL ridges), this one was the most exposed and technical. The talus offers a unique playground of slabs, chimneys, and offwidth to finger cracks along the way. There's more mandatory technical climbing (4th class to 5.4-5) than the South Ridge of Superior, which has more hiking.

The rock felt amazing for most of the route, and wasn't looser than what I would expect from a ridge. But as others have noted, the last 100-200 ft on the summit is a minefield of loose rock ranging from watermelon to trash can size scree/talus. One rock shifting would domino effect the whole mound into moving. You could very easily cause a rock slide here and I wouldn't want to be below anyone on this section. I'd much rather wait on the platform before the upper crux.


Got to come back to explore the "Maybird Palisades" and find a way to attack Thunderbolt Ridge in Hogum!
