The first time I visited Zion back in 2025, I noticed the Watchman. It's one of the first peaks you see looming over the buildings of Springdale as you approach the park's entrance. At first glance, it doesn't look like there's any way to get on top of it's 1,000 to 1,500 ft. cliffs without roping up. But as it turns out, there is one way. The only known scrambling route to the summit goes up the West Face, accesible via a notch in Johnson Mountain ([SummitPost page](https://www.summitpost.org/west-face-scramble/184535)). My friends were doing a road trip down for the weekend, and there was one more open seat, so I tagged along for the ride and good company. I didn't know what I was in for, except that it was going to be an adventure.
\
\
I had my friends drop me off at the trailhead. It was already afternoon, since we had bouldered earlier in the day. I also decided to lug camping gear with me and make it an overnight thing. I didn't want to navigate an unfamiliar maze of cliffs once it got dark.

It wasn't long before I got off track. I began in the wrong spot. The SummitPost said to “start at the end of Canyon Springs Road”, so I began at the end of that road in a culdesac with some nice houses. After bushwhacking, jumping barbed wire, and trespassing for a mile, I decided that I was not on the "climbers' trail" and stopped to get un-lost. 
\
That's when I discovered [this person's topo](https://stavislost.com/hikes/trail/the-watchman-summit/map), which made it obvious that the hike starts out in Canyon Springs **Cove**, not Canyon Springs Road. Bummer. I was tempted to double down on the bushwhack and intersect the trail a mile or so away, but I decided to backtrack. It was a good decision. I quickly found the actual start right [here](https://maps.app.goo.gl/PyZvu3u6ixJtarTx9) (As of March 2026, it was an empty lot, apparently for a future house development, but there wasn't any construction equipment, activity or signs posted. It was chill to walk through, but I'm not sure if I would park in front of it - all of the <span className="special-text">NO TRESPASSING</span> signs in the neighborhood gave me NIMBY vibes). 
\
\
I could have avoided this by downloading an offline map. Instead, I took a lax approach - I was simply comparing my location on Google Maps/Strava to screenshots of the route from SummitPost. It was an oversight that would continue to haunt me over the course of the scramble.

I worked up quite the sweat lugging my tent, some food, extra layers, and a 40oz jug + another 500mL (just under 2L) of water up the west side of the valley, which was baking in afternoon sun. Although I was off-trail for the most part on the upper ridgeline, I was able to find footprints in the sand to stay on track. I also knew the general landmark I was heading towards: the Notch in Johnson mountain that allows access to the west face of the Watchman. For those eager to try, you will want to use the 3rd class ledges trending up and to climbers' left to get to the white arrow in the photos above. Unfortunately, I chose to not go up them the first time around because I didn't see any cairns. I continued all the way to the dark area below the X. 

### Fumbling The Notch
Because I didn't take the 3rd class ledges, I ended up too far south at the base of the wall. Following faint signs that were probably more from deer rather than people, I entered another large notch which had two branches, one north and the other south. Again, if I had downloaded offline maps that showed my position, I would have realized that I was in a completely different notch, and bailed out of that dead end while it was still easy. But I foolishly continued.

The south branch had a 20 ft. chimney with a tricky mantle that looked quite comitting. I retreated to the north branch, but it was completely impassible as well. So, I returned to the south branch thinking that this must be the way. I pulled the 4th class moves and took the gulley as deep as I could, clawing into loose dirt to ascend the steep scree. I reached a dead end at a completely wild grassy shelf. Any more progress was blocked by giant cliffs to the front and steep drops to the side. The lack of any signs of travel should have been screaming clues - but hey, the Watchman is not a well-trafficked route, so it's often a blurry line between trail and bushwhack. But the lack of mention or absence of photos for such a technical section (which turned out to be harder than the actual crux of the scramble) should have tipped me off.

I felt some alarm over having to undo the comitting 4th class move. I crawled back down the scree, moving extremely slowly to avoid a potential slide out and down the chimney. At the lip of the mantle, I took off my pack and chucked it down to the next ledge, and then with utmost finesse and the full range of motion of my shoulders (yoga and deep dips paid off here), reversed the 4th class mantle. I backtracked north and despite not seeing a cairn, took the more obvious 3rd class scrambling up the ledges. I’m not sure if I saw any cairns along the way, but the consistent progress without significant obstacles gave me confidence to keep that path. Eventually, I reached the top of the notch, and recognized the sheltered bivvy spot from [the photographs](https://www.summitpost.org/watchman-scramble-notch/184514/c-184535).

\
As the sun began to set, I continued through the notch and got my first IRL view of the Watchman's West Face.

It was both beautiful and incredibly intimidating. Any remaining desire to continue on to the summit vanished looking at that maze of cliffs and gulleys. At this point I had burned a lot of time from my mistake with the Notch, so I decided to use the remaining daylight to study the face and compare it with my screenshots. I have done cairn quests in the dark before at Lone Peak, and they are the opposite of fun. I’m really glad I didn’t put myself through that.

At dusk, I descended into the hidden valley between the Watchman's West Face and Johnson Mountain, found a way to go skier's left and around the deep trench at the bottom, went over a small hill / ledge, and found myself on a flat sandy area. I pitched my tent in a cactus-free pocket under a tree, just a stone's throw away from the start of the real scramble.

### Camping Thoughts
The camping conditions were sweet that night. Quiet, still, and cool enough to feel cozy in a sleeping bag. It's too bad that I was way too stressed to enjoy it. Looking up from my tent, I could see the silhouettes of the cliffs blotting out the sea of stars above. My fumble with the Notch had me shook, and I was obsessing over the next section, which had much more complicated routefinding. I felt the it in my chest, my thought pattterns, and my heartbeat. I was considering bailing, wondering why I put myself through situations like this. Usually, I don't mind and even enjoy solitude in nature, but I felt very alone on that mountain. I didn't see anyone else the entire time, and the mental challenge and risk changed the experience of being by myself. As I was laying there, I was second guessing why I wasn't just hanging out with my friends, enjoying laughter over a campfire.

\
But it would have been lame to not even try, especially now that I was finally at the start of the real scramble. This was something I've thought about doing for quite a while, and I wasn't going to let a bit of discomfort stop me. I believe that pushing your limits is one of the most rewarding feelings in climbing. When you perform at the limit (or beyond what you thought it was), it's the feeling of winning, of attaining something that can't be bought or taken away. 

\
While that's a healthy thing to pursue when chasing grades in the climbing gym, I'm slowly learning to not bite off more than I can chew when it comes to real mountains, where pushing the limit can have much more severe consequences.

\
Given my background in competition climbing, I rarely find that scrambles challenge my technical climbing limits - the cruxes are usually easier than 5.6. However, the grand scale and variety of terrain offer different types of challenge, like the raw endurance over miles and thousands of feet of vertical gain, route finding, dealing with risk and fear, and the strategy to make sound decisions for all of the above in variable conditions.

### The Summit Push
I rose at 6:30am (an anomaly for me) and left most of my stuff at the tent, so that I could have a speedy summit. As soon as it was bright enough to spot a cairn without a headlamp, I threw 500mL of water, an apple, an extra layer, my InReach, and a power bank into my bag and started off with the excitement of a new day.

### The Descent - Escaping a Maze of Cliffs
Routefinding back down was made difficult by blocked sightlines to cairns, a lack of footprints (the terrain is almost all rock), and a maze of cliffs. 

<figure style={{ textAlign: 'center' }}>
  <img src={watch_10} className="" />
  <figcaption className="blog-caption"></figcaption>
</figure>

Right off the bat, I took a lackadaisical approach to getting down. This sounds really stupid to write right now (because it is), but I didn’t go down the exact way I came up. In my defense, I wasn't on any cairned path for the last section to reach the summit, so I took a different path of least resistance in hopes of spotting a cairn and finding a better way down.
\
I descended a couple of levels down before I realized I was in uncharted territory. I thought I had found another way into an alpine meadow with oak scrub I passed on the way up, but it turned out to be a completely different part of the mountain. I didn't want to climb back up all the way to the summit, which was the last known cairn, but I also feared that  continued efforts to intersect the path again would be a catastrophic waste of energy and time. I was stressed about meeting my friends at a predesignated pickup spot at 9:30am that morning, but that was a small issue I fixed by texting my friends that I was going to be late (there was good service at the top). A way more concerning issue was that my half liter of water was running low. I noticed I was already peeing sunshine yellow the night before, so I was quite dehydrated. 

\
I decided to go back up to the summit, and found the trail again. After crossing point E, which is the alpine meadow, I made consistent progress down the mountain until I hit somewhere between points C and D. I kept going down gulleys that ended up in cliffs, and couldn’t reach the next level below. To make matters worse, I went down the same gulley more than once. I would see that continuing down a gulley looked improbable, turn around and backtrack, but then encounter some cairns (which were probably for lost people) that led me to believe that the gulley was the proper way. I would then go back into the gulley, go deeper until it cliffed out, and then have to backtrack again. At this point, my water was down to \<100mL and I began to feel the effects of dehydration. 
\
A mild panic turned into a more serious, gripping fear that I might be entering a survival situation and that my physical condition could deteriorate further (more light headedness, hyperventilation, poor decision making and coordination leads to rushing tricky moves and sloppy technique, which I noticed happened once or twice). I finished the last of my water, took some deep breaths, and studied the Strava I had saved from my ascent. Strava’s map has detailed elevation bands, so I compared screenshots of my ascent recording to my current recording, and looked at landmark features and elevation bands to position myself vs. the “proper” trail.

From this, I was able to confirm that I was way too far south, and needed to go north. After a weary couple of minutes, I spotted a major cairn and found the path again. 

For the rest of the descent off the Watchman, I used notable landmarks I noticed on the way up to backtrack back down to my camp. For example, I recognized the ascent gulley when I was on top, which indicated to me I was too far north and needed to loop around south to reach the crux and thin ledge again.

### Getting off the mountain
The relief I felt at base camp was compounded with the small bit of extra water I had left there - I definitely should have brought all my water with me for the summit push. Thankfully I was in good enough shape to carefully retrace my steps through the Notch, and back down off Johnson mountain. I did better on the way down to stay on the true trail, now that I knew the general trend of how it went across the landscape (it curves to the north on the way down) and was more attentive to footprints. It was 10am when I finally got off the trail and finished at Canyon Springs Cove.


## Biggest Lessons Learned
This scramble was a lesson in how easily avoidable mistakes can lead to consequences. 
I was able to recover from the mistakes before situations became too dire, like backtracking and reorienting / locating myself on a map.

- always have a full start to finish topo downloaded where you can superimpose your position with the route path, together with detailed topo elevation lines to identify landmark features.
- bring more water. 1.8L for almost 20 hours, 8.6 miles and 6,600 ft. elevation gain was not enough. 3L would have given me some tolerance for route finding delays.
